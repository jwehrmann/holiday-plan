The Shape of the Trip

You land at KEF on Sept 10, 9:25 AM (YVR red-eye) and fly out Sept 29. The route runs clockwise: south coast glaciers → Eastfjords → the North → the Westfjords — and because the Milan leg should never depend on a 6-hour drive, the last night (Sept 28) is a safety stop beside KEF, with a Kirkjufell sunset built into the exit drive. Base logic: Egilsstaðir is the East hub every hike radiates from (Seyðisfjörður is an evening hop over the pass); the Eyjafjörður valley puts Kerling 30 min away and Aldeyjarfoss at 1 h 15; and per your call, Ísafjörður anchors five Westfjords nights — the emptiest, most dramatic corner gets the finale it deserves. Big landmarks are hit at sunrise or late evening; the crowd-free midday hours go to trails tour buses can't reach. Click any hike card for the trail summary, scenic hotspots and a photo gallery.

Base 01

South Coast & Glacier Realm

Sept 10 – 15 · 5 nights
🏠 Airbnb in Kirkjubæjarklaustur (or a rural cabin just east) — the pivot point between the waterfall coast and Vatnajökull's glacier tongues
Σ hiking: ~67 km⛰ ~2,900 m total gain≈ 12 km/day · the biggest block — legs are freshest

Klaustur sits deliberately east of the tour-bus bubble. Icons like Skógafoss and Seljalandsfoss are a dawn strike west; Múlagljúfur, Skaftafell and Jökulsárlón are under an hour east. Stock groceries in Selfoss on arrival day — Klaustur has only a small Kjarval.

Steep, muddy, unsigned — which is exactly why you'll likely have a moss-drenched mega-canyon with two plunging waterfalls to yourselves at dawn. Fog hangs in the gorge on cool September mornings; bring the 60 MP sensor and a polarizer.

Base 02

The Eastfjords

Sept 15 – 19 · 4 nights
🏠 Airbnb in/near Egilsstaðir — the East's road hub; every hike below radiates from it. (Prefer waking up to a fjord? Seyðisfjörður works too, at the cost of a 27 km mountain pass each way, ~+50 min/day.)
Σ hiking: ~46 km⛰ ~1,800 m total gain≈ 11 km/day over a tighter 4-day block

The least-visited quarter of the Ring Road. This is where the trip gets quiet: alpine boulder fields, columnar-basalt canyons, and fjord hikes where the only footprints are reindeer.

Off-beat

Stórurð — The Giant's BouldersTurquoise ponds among house-sized tuff blocks

🚗 ~45 km · 45 min (Vatnsskarð)↔ 10.8 km RT⛰ 607 mModerate+⏱ 4–4.5 h

Under the jagged Dyrfjöll "Door Mountains," a glacial valley strewn with colossal boulders and impossibly bright ponds. Save it for the best-weather day of the block — snow can arrive up here by late September. Poles help on the loose descent.

Base 03

Eyjafjörður & the North

Sept 19 – 23 · 4 nights
🏠 Airbnb/cabin in the Eyjafjörður valley near Akureyri — 30 min to Kerling's trailhead, 1 h 15 to Aldeyjarfoss, 30 min to Goðafoss, a compact block built around one monster summit and one perfect waterfall. (Siglufjörður/Tröllaskagi moved to the Extras list — lovely, but it cost a day the Westfjords now claim.)
Σ hiking: ~26 km⛰ ~1,720 m total gainone huge summit day (Kerling), easy otherwise

The North block is built around one monster summit, one perfect dark-basalt waterfall, and the volcanic weirdness of Mývatn — with a herring-town day and a genuine flex day, because by day 12 your legs will have opinions.

Summit day

Kerling (1,538 m)The highest peak in North Iceland — the trip's crux

🚗 ~25 km · 30 min↔ 12.2 km RT⛰ 1,416 mVery hard⏱ 7–8 h

1,400+ vertical metres of relentless volcanic rubble to the roof of the North — on a clear day you see Eyjafjörður end to end. Expect snow patches up top in late September; hard-shell, gloves, and a strict turnaround time. Total isolation. Total payoff.

AldeyjarfossWhite water through black basalt — your 4x4's reward

🚗 ~75 km · 1 h 15 (last stretch gravel/F26)↔ 2.4 km⛰ 77 mEasy walk⏱ 1 h

The Skjálfandafljót punches through a jet-black colonnade — the best micro-contrast subject in Iceland. The highland approach keeps 95% of visitors away; at sunrise, likely 100%. Pair with Goðafoss on the drive back and Hrafnabjargafoss 4 km further upstream if the road is good.

Base 04

The Westfjords

Sept 23 – 28 · 5 nights
🏠 Airbnb in Ísafjörður — the fjord-locked capital of Iceland's emptiest quarter; Dynjandi 1 h, Kaldbakur ~1 h, Bolafjall 25 min, and essentially zero light pollution
Σ hiking: ~16 km⛰ ~850 m total gainthe deep-solitude block — shorter trails, bigger silences, most scenic driving of the trip

Your call, and the right one for what this trip is about: five nights where the road ends. The Westfjords hold 30% of Iceland's coastline and about 1% of its tourists — in late September you will have thousand-metre fjord walls, a 100 m bridal-veil waterfall and an ocean-cliff summit essentially to yourselves. The trade was Snæfellsnes (preserved as the alternative) — and its one must-see, Kirkjufell, survives as a built-in sunset stop on the Sept 28 exit drive.

★ AnchorSunset

DynjandiThe bridal-veil giant of the fjords

🚗 ~60 km · 1 h from Ísafjörður↔ 1.6 km⛰ 92 mShort + steep⏱ 40 min

100 m wide at its base, seven falls stacked beneath it, facing west into the sunset over Arnarfjörður. The climb past the lower falls layers composition after composition.

Base 05 · Safety stop

Keflavík — the Breather

Sept 28 – 29 · 1 night
🏠 Airbnb in Keflavík / Njarðvík — 10 minutes from the terminal. The Milan leg starts rested, fueled, and with zero dependence on Westfjords roads or weather.

Sept 28 is the farewell tour, not a slog: Ísafjörður → the fjord road south → Snæfellsnes → Kirkjufell & Kirkjufellsfoss at golden hour (★ your must-see, 5 min off Route 54) → Gerðuberg basalt columns if light remains → Keflavík by ~21:30. Total ~640 km, ~7.5 h of driving broken by the best photo stop in the country. On the 29th: coffee, 10-minute drive, drop the 4x4, board for Milan.

★ MustSunrise

Kirkjufell & KirkjufellsfossThe most photographed mountain in Iceland — at 6:45 AM, yours

🚗 2 km · 5 min↔ 3.5 km⛰ 16 mEasy⏱ 1 h

Living 5 minutes away is the cheat code: walk over for sunrise before any tour van leaves Reykjavík, and come back on any aurora night — the arrowhead peak under green sky is the classic for a reason. Waterfall-foreground comp works best with the tide-fed pools still.

Alternative Base 04

The Snæfellsnes Version

Sept 23/25 – 28 · Grundarfjörður
Preserved as the weather escape hatch: if the ~Sept 18 long-range forecast shows the Westfjords socked in for your whole window, swap this block back in — Grundarfjörður base, everything below ready to go, and the KEF safety night still applies (it's only 2.5 h out). Challenge

Helgrindur (Lýsuhyrna approach)The peninsula's wild, unmarked ridge — real mountaineering-lite

🚗 ~25 km · 25 min↔ ~9 km RT⛰ ~900 mVery hard · unmarked⏱ 5–6 h

Provisions, Fuel & Survival Logistics

🛒 Food strategy

  • Rule: big-shop at the cheap chains (Bónus, Krónan, Nettó) at each base handover; rural stores are sparse and pricey.
  • Day 1: Krónan/Bónus Selfoss — 5 days of food before Klaustur (only a small Kjarval there).
  • Sept 15: Nettó/Bónus Egilsstaðir.
  • Sept 19/20: Bónus Akureyri (biggest selection outside Reykjavík).
  • Sept 23: Bónus Ísafjörður — load 5 nights; village stores beyond are tiny.
  • Hike food: skyr, flatkökur, dried fish, chocolate, instant soup. Pack lunch every day — trailhead cafés basically don't exist.

⛽ Fuel discipline

  • Rule of thumb: never below half a tank. Gaps between stations in the East and Westfjords exceed 100 km.
  • Get an N1 key fob / Orkan app — most rural pumps are unmanned, card-only, and some reject foreign cards without a PIN.
  • Critical fill-ups: Selfoss → Vík → Klaustur → Höfn → Egilsstaðir → Reykjahlíð (Mývatn) → Akureyri → Blönduós → Hólmavík → Ísafjörður → (Sept 28) Þingeyri → Búðardalur/Borgarnes.
  • Budget: ~3,200 km × ~9 L/100km 4x4 ≈ 290 L ≈ 88,000 ISK (~$630).

💧 Water & kit

  • Tap water is glacier-grade everywhere. Carry two 1.5 L bottles each, refill daily; a thermos of hot drink earns its weight on summit days. (Ignore the sulfur smell in hot taps — cold is pristine.)
  • Streams above farms are drinkable on trail; glacial (milky) water is not.
  • Layers: hard-shell top+bottom, insulation, hat/gloves — Kerling and Kaldbakur can be winter up top in late Sept.
  • Microspikes (light ones) for early-morning frost near falls; headlamps for pre-dawn starts.
  • Offline: download Iceland in Google Maps + AllTrails/Wikiloc tracks; cell coverage dies in exactly the places you're going.

🚙 Driving & safety

  • 4x4 needed for: Þakgil road, Aldeyjarfoss (F26 southern stretch), Stuðlagil east parking gravel, and the Kvennaskarð track to Kaldbakur's trailhead.
  • Check road.is + vedur.is every morning; safetravel.is trip plan before Kerling and Kaldbakur.
  • September = first storms. Hold doors against wind gusts (the classic rental damage) and never drive in a yellow/red weather warning.
  • Headlights on 24/7 (law). Sheep own the road.
🌋 Volcano watch (as of July 2026): no eruption is active right now — the last Sundhnúkur (Reykjanes) eruption ended in Aug 2025 — but magma is re-accumulating under Svartsengi and the Met Office rates a new eruption as likely. The whole eruption zone sits 20 min from KEF, far from every base in this plan, so it's zero risk to the itinerary — and if a fissure opens while you're here, it's a once-in-a-lifetime bolt-on for arrival or departure day. Check en.vedur.is and safetravel.is for status and legal viewpoints; never freelance onto fresh lava fields.

September Weather — What to Actually Expect

Long-range forecasts for specific September 2026 days don't exist yet — Iceland's weather is only skillfully forecast ~5–7 days out (vedur.is). What's dependable is the climatology: September is the hinge month between mild autumn and the first Atlantic storms. Temperatures fall ~2°C over your 19 days, the first storm systems typically arrive after mid-month, and snow begins dusting peaks above ~800 m — which is exactly where Kerling and Kaldbakur live. For reference, September 2025 ran ~0.5–1°C above these norms.

Base (dates)Day / night tempRainCharacter
1 · South Coast (Sept 10–15)~10–11°C / 5–6°C~140–170 mm · rain on 15–18 daysThe wettest, mildest region — Atlantic-facing. Expect showers most days, dramatic clearing light between them (a gift for the camera), and strong gusts near Reynisfjara/Dyrhólaey. Early Sept = statistically calmest of your trip.
2 · Eastfjords (Sept 15–20)~9–10°C / 3–5°C~90–100 mm · 12–15 daysOften the driest quarter in westerly flow — Egilsstaðir sits in a rain shadow and gets Iceland's best föhn-warmed afternoons. Stórurð (600 m+) can see its first snow dusting late Sept.
3 · North (Sept 20–25)~8–9°C / 2–4°C~55–75 mm · the fewest rain daysDriest region, coldest nights — which also means the best aurora odds when skies clear. Kerling's upper 500 m is genuinely alpine by now: snow patches, ice-glazed rock, winter kit non-negotiable.
4 · Westfjords (Sept 23–28)~8–9°C / 3–4°C~120–140 mm · 15–17 daysRaw and changeable — fjord walls make their own weather, and late-Sept lows arrive here first. Wind is the go/no-go call for Kaldbakur; Dynjandi and the town days work in anything. Mountain passes can see the season's first snow.
5 · Keflavík (Sept 28–29)~9–10°C / 5–6°C—One night; the point is precisely that weather no longer matters.
How to run it in practice: every evening check vedur.is (text forecast + wind map) and belgingur.is; rank the next 3 days and slot hikes accordingly — summits on the calm day, canyons and waterfalls in the drizzle, driving on the ugly day. Yellow warning = reshuffle; orange/red = nobody drives. Rain here is usually sideways drizzle in 10–15 m/s wind rather than downpours: full hard-shell armor beats an umbrella (which Iceland eats for breakfast). And the classic September pattern — 4 grey days, then one impossibly clear one — is exactly why every base has a flex day.

Light & Sky Calendar

🌅 Daylight windows

  • Sept 10 (Klaustur): sunrise 6:22, sunset 19:59 — 13.6 h of light.
  • Sept 29 (Keflavík): sunrise 7:34, sunset 19:10 — 11.6 h. You lose ~6 min/day; full table below.
  • Golden hour is long and lazy at this latitude — roughly 1.5 h after sunrise and before sunset.
  • Every "sunrise strike" in this plan means arriving at the trailhead/falls ~30 min before the listed sunrise.

🌌 Aurora playbook

  • New moon Sept 11, full moon Sept 26 — darkest skies land in Bases 1–2; late-trip nights are moonlit (still shootable, different look).
  • Nightly ritual: check the vedur.is aurora forecast (Kp + cloud map) at ~21:00; chase gaps in cloud, not Kp numbers.
  • Pre-scouted foregrounds: Jökulsárlón (B1), Stuðlagil rim / Seyðisfjörður (B2), Goðafoss & Mývatn (B3), the fjord mouth & Dynjandi (B4), Kirkjufell at sunset + Garður lighthouse (Sept 28).
  • Settings baseline: 14–20 mm, f/1.8–2.8, 5–10 s, ISO 1600–6400 (drop ISO under moonlight).

📷 a7RV field notes

  • Waterfall spray is relentless: 3+ microfiber cloths, a rocket blower, and a rain cover; wipe between every frame at Skógafoss/Dettifoss.
  • Polarizer for moss/canyon work (Múlagljúfur, Fjaðrárgljúfur); 6-stop ND for silky water at Aldeyjarfoss/Goðafoss.
  • Cold kills batteries ~30% faster — keep spares in an inside pocket; 60 MP files eat cards, bring doubles.
  • Crowd-dodging summary: icons at dawn or after 18:00; unmarked trails at midday; nobody is anywhere before 8 AM in September.
